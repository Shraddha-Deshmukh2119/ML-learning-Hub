Gaussian Mixture Models

Probabilistic models for representing normally distributed clusters

What are Gaussian Mixture Models?

Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) are probabilistic models that assume all data points are generated from a mixture of a finite number of Gaussian distributions with unknown parameters. GMMs are a form of soft clustering where each data point has a probability of belonging to each cluster.

Unlike K-means, which assigns each point to exactly one cluster, GMMs allow points to belong to multiple clusters with different degrees of membership. This makes GMMs more flexible and capable of capturing complex structures in the data.

Mathematically, a Gaussian mixture model is represented as:

p(x) = Σₖ πₖ N(x|μₖ,Σₖ)

Where:

  • p(x) is the probability density of the data point x
  • πₖ is the weight of the k-th mixture component (must sum to 1)
  • N(x|μₖ,Σₖ) is the Gaussian probability density with mean μₖ and covariance Σₖ
  • K is the number of mixture components (clusters)

Learning Parameters:

The parameters of a GMM (means, covariances, and mixture weights) are typically estimated using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, which iteratively:

  1. E-step: Computes the probability that each data point belongs to each cluster
  2. M-step: Updates the parameters based on these probabilities

Advantages:

  • Provides soft assignments of data points to clusters
  • Can model clusters of different sizes and shapes
  • Offers a measure of uncertainty through probabilities
  • Can detect outliers (points with low probability under the model)
  • Works well for mixed distributions

Applications:

  • Image segmentation
  • Speaker recognition and verification
  • Anomaly detection
  • Financial modeling
  • Biometric identification
  • Text document clustering
